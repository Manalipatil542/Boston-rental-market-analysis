Project Overview

How can you tell if you're getting ripped off?

Boston consistently ranks among the most expensive cities in the United States to rent an apartment. Many factors determine rental prices, and apartment-seekers could benefit from a simple, objective metric to evaluate whether the asking price of a listing is justified. Put simply — how can I tell if I'm getting ripped off?

This project analyzes 500 Boston rental listings across 16 neighborhoods with 28 attributes per listing — including price, square footage, bedrooms, bathrooms, MBTA proximity, distance to major universities, amenities, and days on market.

The project had three overall goals:

🎯
Goal 1 — Understand the Market
Analyze the distribution of rental prices across Boston neighborhoods, identify the most and least affordable areas, and uncover which factors drive rent prices.
🤖
Goal 2 — Build a Price Prediction Model
Develop a machine learning model that predicts expected rent based on property characteristics and location, achieving R²=0.982 and MAE=$97/month.
💡
Goal 3 — Generate Actionable Insights
Categorize every listing as Excellent Deal, Good Deal, Fair Price, Overpriced, or Very Overpriced — and identify where renters can find genuine value in Boston's competitive market.

Section 1 — Data Overview

Dataset Description

The dataset contains 500 Boston rental listings with 28 attributes per listing. Variables were organized into four categories: property attributes, location features, amenity features, and derived pricing features.

Property Attributes

Key property variables include price (monthly rent in USD), number of bedrooms (0–4), bathrooms, square footage, and price per square foot. The dataset also records days on market, which serves as a proxy for listing demand — units with fewer days on market indicate stronger demand.

Location Features

Each listing includes neighborhood (16 distinct areas), MBTA line assignment (Red, Orange, Green, or Blue), distance to the nearest MBTA station in miles, and distances to four major universities: Harvard, MIT, Boston University, and Northeastern.

Amenity Features

Amenity data covers heat included (39.0% of listings), hot water included (35.8%), parking included (26.8%), pets allowed (46.2%), laundry type (in-unit, in-building, or none), furnished status, and broker fee. A composite amenity score from 0 to 8 was derived from these binary features.

Derived Features

The most important derived features are the model's predicted price and the resulting price difference percentage — the percentage by which a listing's actual price exceeds or falls short of the model's expected price. This forms the basis of the deal scoring system.

"The overall distribution of prices was positively skewed (skewness = 0.721), with the majority of listings concentrated between $2,700 and $4,500 per month. The distribution reflects Boston's housing market: a large middle tier of standard apartments with a long upper tail driven by luxury units in premium neighborhoods."
Figure 1
Distribution of Monthly Rent — Price by Bedroom Count

Figure 1. Average monthly rent by bedroom count across all 500 listings. Bedroom count is the strongest single predictor of rent (r=0.825). Each additional bedroom adds approximately $1,000–$1,100/month, with studios averaging $2,061 and 4-bedroom units averaging $6,165 — a nearly 3× difference.

The data also reveals a strong interaction between property size and price. For listings that included square footage, there was a strong positive correlation (r=0.704) between sqft and price, suggesting that price per square foot varies meaningfully across neighborhoods. Larger units in premium neighborhoods command both higher absolute prices and higher price-per-sqft.

Section 2 — Neighborhood Analysis

A $2,164 gap separates best from worst value

Average rent varies dramatically by neighborhood. Back Bay ($4,692/month) and Beacon Hill ($4,668/month) represent the premium tier — driven by central location, historic prestige, and proximity to the Financial District. At the other end, Roxbury ($2,528/month) and East Boston ($2,815/month) offer dramatically lower rents while maintaining strong MBTA access.

The analysis groups neighborhoods into three tiers based on average monthly rent:

🏙️
Premium Tier (avg > $4,000/month)
Back Bay, Beacon Hill, Cambridge, South Boston, South End, Brookline — central locations with high demand, limited supply, and luxury housing stock.
🏘️
Mid-Tier ($3,000–$4,000/month)
Somerville, Fenway, Charlestown, Jamaica Plain, Allston, Mission Hill, Brighton — the bulk of the market with diverse housing types and good transit access.
💚
Value Tier (avg < $3,000/month)
Dorchester, East Boston, Roxbury — most affordable neighborhoods with growing demand and strong appreciation potential.

A particularly notable finding is East Boston's position in the dataset. Despite being directly on the Blue Line with convenient access to downtown Boston, East Boston has the second-lowest average rent ($2,815/month) and the highest listing count (42 listings) of any neighborhood. This suggests that East Boston is significantly undervalued relative to its transit convenience and represents one of the best opportunities for value-conscious renters in the dataset.

Section 3 — Price Prediction Model

A model that explains 98.2% of rent variation

The core analytical contribution of this project is a machine learning model that predicts expected rental price based on property characteristics and location. The model enables systematic, objective identification of underpriced and overpriced listings across the entire Boston market.

Feature Analysis

Prior to modeling, I analyzed the correlation between each numeric feature and rental price to understand which factors drive Boston rents. The results reveal a clear hierarchy of predictors, with property size features dominating and location features playing a surprisingly secondary role.

Figure 3
Feature Correlations with Monthly Rent

Figure 3. Pearson correlation coefficients between numeric features and monthly rent. Bedroom count (r=0.825) and square footage (r=0.704) are the dominant predictors. MBTA distance shows only a weak negative correlation (-0.151), challenging the conventional assumption that transit proximity strongly drives Boston rent prices.

The dominance of bedroom count (r=0.825) and square footage (r=0.704) confirms that property size is the primary pricing driver in Boston. The surprisingly weak correlation for MBTA proximity (-0.151) and near-zero correlations for university distances (all below 0.05) challenge conventional wisdom about Boston rental pricing. Neighborhood brand and apartment size matter far more than transit access when landlords set prices.

The amenity score's low correlation (0.098) is also notable — it suggests that landlords are not efficiently pricing amenities like heat, parking, and in-unit laundry into their rents. This represents a systematic opportunity for renters: listings that include these amenities are effectively offering them for free relative to market expectations.

Model Performance

The price prediction model was evaluated on the full dataset of 500 listings and achieved exceptional performance metrics:

0.982
R-squared Score
The model explains 98.2% of variance in rental prices across 500 Boston listings — an exceptionally strong fit.
$97
Mean Absolute Error / Month
Predictions are within $97 of actual price on average — just 2.6% of mean rent. Reliable enough to flag any 10%+ deviation.

An R² of 0.982 indicates an exceptionally strong model fit. The low MAE of $97/month means that for a typical $3,711/month listing, the model's prediction is off by only 2.6% — well within the margin needed to reliably identify genuine deals versus fairly priced units. This provides a solid basis for the deal scoring system described in the next section.

Section 4 — Deal Score Analysis

Only 9.4% of listings are genuine good deals

Each listing was categorized based on the percentage difference between actual price and model-predicted price. This creates a standardized, objective deal score that allows renters to compare listings across neighborhoods and property types on equal footing. The categories were defined as follows:

✅
Excellent Deal — 16 listings (3.2%)
Priced 10% or more below predicted value. These are rare mispriced listings representing significant savings versus comparable units.
👍
Good Deal — 31 listings (6.2%)
Priced below predicted value. Worth prioritizing in your apartment search — below market but within normal variance.
⚖️
Fair Price — 426 listings (85.2%)
Priced within the expected range for the property's characteristics and location. The vast majority of the market.
⚠️
Overpriced / Very Overpriced — 27 listings (5.4%)
Priced above predicted value, with Very Overpriced listings at 10%+ above expected price. These should be avoided or negotiated down aggressively.
Figure 5
Deal Distribution & Best Neighborhoods for Good Deals

Figure 5. Left: Distribution of listing categories showing 85.2% of listings are fairly priced — deals are rare in Boston's competitive market. Right: Top neighborhoods by count of Good and Excellent Deal listings. Roxbury leads with 9 good deals, followed by Mission Hill (6), East Boston and Brighton (5 each).

The market is overwhelmingly composed of fairly priced listings (85.2%), reflecting a tight, landlord-favored rental market where competition naturally eliminates significant mispricing. Only 9.4% of listings represent genuine good deals — making systematic deal identification a high-value analytical problem for renters.

Importantly, Very Overpriced listings appear across all neighborhood tiers — from premium Back Bay to affordable Roxbury. This confirms that overpricing is driven by individual landlord decisions rather than neighborhood-level market dynamics. Using the model as a benchmark is therefore essential regardless of which neighborhood you are searching in.

Section 5 — Transit & Location Analysis

Transit proximity: a surprisingly weak price driver

Boston's MBTA system is often cited as a major driver of rental prices. The data tells a more nuanced story. While transit proximity does show a slight negative correlation with price (-0.151), this effect is weak and dominated by neighborhood and property size effects. Renters should not assume that living further from a station will yield significant savings.

Figure 6
Average Rent by MBTA Line

Figure 6. Average monthly rent for listings served by each MBTA line. Red Line neighborhoods command the highest average rents ($4,134/month), but all four lines have nearly identical average distances to stations (0.28–0.33 miles). The rent difference reflects neighborhood prestige — Cambridge, South Boston and Beacon Hill for the Red Line vs. East Boston for the Blue Line — not transit access per se.

The data also reveals that distances to major universities (Harvard, MIT, BU, Northeastern) have essentially negligible correlations with rent (all below ±0.05). This is counterintuitive — one would expect university proximity to command a premium. The likely explanation is that university areas like Cambridge, Fenway and Mission Hill are already captured as high-demand neighborhoods, meaning the university proximity effect is absorbed into the neighborhood variable rather than adding an independent premium.

💡
Key Insight: East Boston is the Market's Hidden Gem
East Boston sits directly on the Blue Line with a 10–15 minute commute to downtown Boston. Yet it has the 2nd lowest average rent ($2,815/month) in the dataset — 24% below the city average — and the highest listing count (42 listings) of any neighborhood. For commuters willing to cross the harbor, East Boston offers exceptional value that the market has not yet fully priced in.
Section 6 — Conclusions

Key findings & recommendations

This project combined exploratory data analysis, feature correlation analysis, and machine learning to extract actionable insights from 500 Boston rental listings. The results provide a clear, data-driven framework for evaluating rental listings in one of America's most expensive cities.

Model Limitations & Future Work

The dataset covers 500 listings at a single point in time, meaning seasonal variation and longer-term price trends are not captured. The model also does not account for qualitative factors such as unit condition, building age, natural light, or views — which can significantly affect perceived value and willingness to pay.

Future work could improve prediction accuracy by incorporating neighborhood-level time series data, testing nonlinear models, and building a live web application — similar to the RentNYC approach — that allows non-technical users to compare expected and actual prices for individual listings with a single click.
